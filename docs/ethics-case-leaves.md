CASE: The Reward Rewire
*What comes after an app that only refuses to log the wrong hobby?*

Leaves is a real habit-tracking app built for students: plant a "tree" for anything you love — piano, basketball, watercolor — log time toward it, and watch it grow. It also runs a keyword filter that refuses to let a student log "TikTok" or "Genshin Impact" as a tracked hobby, on the reasoning that an app built to fight screen addiction shouldn't quietly reward it. The filter is trivial to defeat: a student just doesn't type the banned word.

Now imagine the same team's next product. NeuroLeaf is a thin adhesive patch worn behind the ear, using the same closed-loop neurostimulation already demonstrated in real human deep-brain-stimulation trials for addiction [1] and the same brain-signal read-and-write capability Neuralink has shown in paralysis patients [2]. NeuroLeaf doesn't ask what app is open. It reads the dopamine-linked reward signal directly and acts on it in real time: the instant a short-video feed loads, it dampens that signal, so the scroll simply feels flat and unsatisfying. The instant the student picks up a violin or a basketball, it amplifies the same signal, so the hobby feels unusually, chemically good. Parents opt their child in during setup, the same way they'd approve a screen-time limit — except this limit is enforced inside the child's own skull, and it doesn't just block an app, it changes what the child likes.

The company markets it in the same language as the original filter: protecting kids from platforms already engineered to manipulate their dopamine system without consent. Its founders argue they are not inventing manipulation, only correcting an existing one, with a parent's informed opt-in, more precisely than any keyword filter ever could — the emerging real-world use of closed-loop stimulation to treat compulsive behavior is exactly this same logic, applied to addiction that's already diagnosed [1].

Critics see a line being crossed that a text filter never approached. Refusing to log an activity leaves a child's judgment intact; they can still find TikTok funny, still choose to open it, still resent the app for not counting it. NeuroLeaf removes the choice at its root by rewriting what pleasure itself attaches to. A child conditioned to feel numb around a screen and unusually euphoric around a violin has not chosen music — their reward circuitry has been edited by a device they never had the standing to consent to themselves. If TikTok manipulating a child's attention from the outside is the harm the entire industry is currently on trial for [3], engineering the identical manipulation from the inside, even in the opposite direction, is not obviously a correction. It may be the same violation wearing a halo.

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS

1. If a device manipulates the exact neural pathway that addictive apps already manipulate, just toward a healthier outcome, is that ethically equivalent to what those apps do, or does the direction of the manipulation change everything?
2. Can a parent ethically consent, on a minor's behalf, to a device that alters what that child finds pleasurable, rather than merely what they're allowed to access?
3. Is there a meaningful ethical line between an app that refuses to *log* an activity and a device that makes a child physically unable to *enjoy* it — and if so, where exactly does that line sit?
